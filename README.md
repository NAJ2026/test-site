<p>I have worked with both physical and digital publishing; however, I have worked more with digital formats in recent years. First, things like video creation, audio recording, and social media have been made accessible to me more than physical publishing. I have only been involved with two so far: 
<UL> 
<LI> Sheepshead Review as an editor
<LI> Northern Lights as a writer
</UL>
<a title="Mossytravels05, CC BY 4.0 &lt;https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0&gt;, via Wikimedia Commons" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sheepshead_and_Northern_Lights_Issues.jpg"><img width="256" alt="Image of two small journals, one of Sheepshead Review and the other of Northern Lights" src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sheepshead_and_Northern_Lights_Issues.jpg/256px-Sheepshead_and_Northern_Lights_Issues.jpg?_=20260307224004"></a>
</p>

<p>Physical media takes time; social media is <b>free</b> and <b>immediate</b>. I can put anything I want up on Instagram without permission from anyone else. However, my audience is limited to family, friends, and random viewers who find my work by hashtag.
</p>

<p>
<blockquote>"The scariest moment is always just before you start."
	- A sticky note my parents gave me
    </blockquote>
</p>

<p>Digital publishing has also made me feel more competitive. Yes, I can make whatever I want and release it to the ether of the Internet, but so can everyone else. I can publish <i>anything</i>, but should I publish <i>everything</i>? Probably not. Some things are just meant for me.
</p>
